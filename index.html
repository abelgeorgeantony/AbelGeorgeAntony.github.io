Waypoints in my life

Life before programming
I was born on the twenty-second of April, two-thousand five in Kerala, India. It is very hard for me to recall the past(people mock me by saying I have alzheimers!). I went to Georgian public school till 2nd standard and moved to trivandrum for my father's professional needs. There I joined [school name]. I attended a total of one academical hour in that school that's when my mother got me out of the school! My father had met with an accident and on our way to meet him in the hospital he passed away. After that because there was nobody to look after us in trivandrum we went to live with my mother's brother in bangalore. There I completed 3rd standard and came back to kerala to live with my father's family. I attended Georgian public school till 7th standard and joined St. Aloysius in 8th standard. In 2021 I passed SSLC with 93% in terms of grades. Higher Secondary was available in the same school, So I joined for computer science there.
First steps
My journey as a programmer began in 11th grade when I had no prior knowledge of programming and limited understanding of computers. However, I was always fascinated by technology, which led me to choose the computer science stream in school. Around the same time, I stumbled upon an advertisement in a video for the 100k coding challenge by the channel Brototype (previously known as Crossroads). Intrigued by the video, I watched the intro and signed up for the challenge. Unfortunately, due to my lacking discipline and without access to a computer, I didn't make much progress at that time. However, my determination to learn programming led me to discover a mobile application called Termux. I started using it to learn and run C programs from the 100k challenge. This mobile app provided me with a platform to practice coding even without a computer. As time went on, I faced a roadblock in my progress with the 100k challenge. The challenge began focusing on teaching Java and its Swing library, which required a computer. Since I didn't have one, I felt stuck and unable to proceed. Despite the setback, I refused to give up. Instead, I started experimenting on my own by writing terminal-level applications in C++. One of my proudest creations during this time was a program that printed a yearly calendar. Eventually, I gained access to my uncle's old desktop computer, which had Windows 7 installed on it, although it was slowly becoming obsolete. Despite the challenges, I persevered and eventually completed the 100k challenge. I continued my self-guided learning journey by exploring topics on the internet, often staying ahead of or in sync with the teachings in my school's computer classes. During computer lab sessions, I found joy in helping my fellow students when they encountered errors or needed explanations of concepts, especially during exam preparation. This experience made me feel like an expert in computers and programming to my peers. However, it is important to note that while I excelled in programming, my academic grades did not reflect my expertise.
Second steps
In my 11th-grade, I achieved a total score of 53%. The following year, in 12th grade, my score improved to 69%. Despite the societal pressure and expectations to excel academically, my family's surprise at my passing grades was evident. I hadn't been a diligent student except during exams. Recognizing my academic performance, I came to terms with the misalignment between my grades and my programming passion. Initially planning to enroll in a coding camp, my family disagreed with non-degree learning. As a compromise, I opted for a Bachelor of Computer Applications (BCA) degree that resonated with my interests, albeit involving subjects like mathematics and English. Family pressure led me to apply online for colleges, ambitiously seeking admission to several. One of these institutions conducted an interview and ghosted me, while another rejected me based on my low grades, suggesting the challenging nature of BCA made my aspiration impractical. Despite initial discouragement due to my marks, Bharat Mata College of Commerce and Arts (BMCCA) eventually granted me admission. Although smaller, BMCCA's peaceful environment felt like a victory considering the circumstances.
Now what?
Thus, I am now continuing on my journey as a programmer, fueled by passion and determination, with my admission to BMCCA.
